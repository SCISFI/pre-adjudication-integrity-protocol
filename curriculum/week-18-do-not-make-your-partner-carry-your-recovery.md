# Week 18 — Do Not Make Your Partner Carry Your Recovery

## Orientation

This week is about refusing to make your spouse, partner, family member, or affected person carry your recovery.

The goal is to build a recovery structure that does not depend on her monitoring, comforting, managing, supervising, forgiving, or reassuring you.

## Core Teaching

When a man is exposed, accused, arrested, removed from the home, or facing sexual behavior consequences, he may desperately want someone close to him to stabilize him.

If he has a spouse or partner, he may want her to become:

- his therapist
- his probation officer
- his emotional regulator
- his accountability partner
- his proof that he is changing
- his comfort source
- his confession container
- his crisis manager
- his reassurance provider
- his path back to normal

That is not responsibility.

That is transferring the weight of recovery onto the person who may already be injured, frightened, angry, betrayed, or overwhelmed.

Your recovery must be carried by an appropriate structure: licensed therapist, supervised clinical provider, clinical supervisor when relevant, attorney for legal matters, group support if appropriate, recovery supports, daily honesty, boundaries, and your own willingness to do the work.

Your spouse or partner may choose to observe, participate in appropriate clinical processes, set boundaries, ask questions, separate, stay, leave, grieve, rage, or go silent. Those choices belong to her.

Your work is to stop making her responsible for whether you stay grounded.

## Reality Check

If she has to monitor you for you to stay honest, you are not practicing ownership.

If she has to comfort you before you can function, you are not carrying your recovery.

If she becomes your crisis container, you are placing weight on the wrong person.

If you use her pain as proof that you might as well give up, you are avoiding responsibility.
