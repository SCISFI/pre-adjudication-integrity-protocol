# Week 13 — Digital Boundaries Are Recovery Boundaries

## Orientation

This week is about digital boundaries.

The goal is not to turn technology into the enemy or the app into a surveillance tool. The goal is to recognize that devices, apps, browsers, accounts, passwords, internet access, and hidden digital spaces can either support integrity or protect secrecy.

## Core Teaching

For many men, the digital world became the place where secrecy lived.

A phone, laptop, browser, social media account, search history, private folder, hidden app, second email account, old password, or late-night routine may have carried more than convenience. It may have carried access, secrecy, fantasy, pornography, compartmentalization, and escalation.

Digital boundaries are not punishment. They are recovery structure.

A man in serious recovery does not ask, “How much access can I keep?”

He asks, “What access has been connected to secrecy, and what structure is needed to stop protecting it?”

Digital boundaries may include:

- removing private browser access
- eliminating secret accounts
- changing device routines
- limiting unsupervised nighttime access
- using accountability software when clinically appropriate
- giving up devices or platforms that supported secrecy
- following attorney, court, bond, DSS/CPS, or safety restrictions
- bringing digital patterns into therapy
- refusing to negotiate with loopholes

This app does not monitor your devices. It does not track usage. It does not detect relapse. It asks you to tell the truth about the digital conditions that either support or weaken integrity.

## Reality Check

If you keep the same access, the same privacy, the same secrecy, and the same routines, you should not expect a different outcome.

Digital freedom without integrity is not freedom. It is exposure waiting for opportunity.

A man who wants to change stops asking, “Can I technically access this?”

He starts asking, “Would keeping this access protect the old system?”

## Relationship / Family Reflection

If you have a spouse or partner, do not ask her to feel safe simply because you say you are trying. Digital safety requires visible consistency, clear boundaries, and a willingness to give up access that protected secrecy.

If you are single or not partnered, consider what digital boundaries are necessary for future relational integrity, family trust, and self-respect.
