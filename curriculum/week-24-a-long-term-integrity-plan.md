# Week 24 — A Long-Term Integrity Plan

## Orientation

This week is about building a long-term integrity plan.

The goal is not to “graduate” from responsibility. The goal is to leave this phase with a clear structure for the next twelve months of recovery, support, boundaries, and accountability.

## Core Teaching

A man does not finish integrity work because he completes a program.

Completion is not proof of safety. It is not proof of rehabilitation. It is not a legal conclusion. It is not a treatment discharge. It is not a guarantee that the future will be restored.

Completion means you have participated in structured reflection and accountability work.

The next question is:

> What structure will hold me when this program is no longer new?

A long-term integrity plan should address:

- licensed therapy or supervised clinical care
- group support if appropriate
- digital boundaries
- device and internet structure
- emotional vulnerability patterns
- pornography and fantasy boundaries
- spouse/partner or family boundaries
- legal and safety compliance
- crisis plan
- support contact rhythm
- daily honesty practices
- relapse-prevention work with a clinician
- loneliness and male bonding
- accountability density
- meaningful service, work, and community

The plan should be specific enough to practice and humble enough to revise.

## Reality Check

If your plan is vague, your old system will find the gaps.

If your plan depends on willpower, it is too weak.

If your plan depends on someone else monitoring you, it is not ownership.

If your plan ignores loneliness, boredom, shame, digital access, and secrecy, it is incomplete.

Long-term integrity requires structure before temptation, honesty before exposure, and support before collapse.

## Legal and Clinical Boundary Reminder

Do not enter offense details, victim names, investigative facts, illegal content descriptions, police facts, or legal strategy. Use this space for recovery reflection, accountability, boundaries, emotions, and next right actions. Discuss legal details with your attorney and clinical details with your licensed therapist or supervised clinical provider.

## Reflection Questions

1. What truth am I facing this week?
2. Where am I tempted to hide, minimize, blame, or manage my image?
3. What emotion is most dangerous for me right now?
4. What boundary do I need to honor this week?
5. Who needs to know how I am really doing?
6. What do I need to discuss with my licensed therapist or supervised clinical provider?
7. What is my one integrity commitment this week?

## Relationship / Family Reflection

If you have a spouse or partner, do not use your long-term plan to demand hope. Use it to practice consistency, safety, humility, and accountability whether reconciliation occurs or not.

If you are single or not partnered, design a plan that protects future relationships before they begin.

## Therapist Prompt

Bring your long-term integrity plan to therapy. Ask your licensed therapist or supervised clinical provider what is missing, unrealistic, too vague, or clinically important to strengthen.
